06 — Key Findings

What the Analysis Reveals

// Synthesized analytical conclusions drawn from all six visualizations, suitable for presentation to stakeholders or academic submission.

–0.67
Strong Negative Correlation: Screen Time ↔ Sleep
Daily phone hours and sleep duration show a robust inverse relationship (r = –0.67). Each additional hour of screen time is associated with approximately 11 fewer minutes of sleep — a compounding deficit over weeks.
+42%
Social Media Dominates Screen Time Budget
Social media apps account for 42% of total daily screen time on average, exceeding work productivity usage (28%). This passive consumption pattern directly competes with sleep and recovery time.
3×
Smartphone Users: Highest Stress Concentration
High-stress individuals (score ≥ 7) are 3× more concentrated among smartphone-primary users vs desktop-primary users — suggesting always-on connectivity as a key stress driver.
8.3h
Students Lead in Daily Phone Hours
Students report the highest daily phone usage (8.3h average), followed by remote workers (8.1h). Both groups also show below-average sleep duration and above-average stress, forming a concerning triad.
8h
The 8-Hour Threshold: Productivity Cliff
Work productivity scores decline sharply at the 8h daily screen time mark — dropping an average of 18 points compared to users in the 2–4h range. Beyond 10h, scores fall below 45/100.
+0.8
Caffeine as a Proxy for Sleep Debt
High screen time groups consume 0.8 more cups of caffeine daily than low screen time groups, suggesting compensatory stimulant use to counteract sleep deprivation — a behavioral cycle that further disrupts sleep architecture.
